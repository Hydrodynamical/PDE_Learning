\title{\textbf{Can Language Models Learn a PDE?}\\[4pt]
\large A Cognitive Benchmark for Scientific Reasoning\\via Weak-Form Identification of an Unknown Elliptic Equation}
\author{}
\date{}

\begin{abstract}
We present a benchmark that measures scientific reasoning in large language
models through the task of identifying an unknown elliptic PDE from its
solution.  The model receives solution data, a budget of queries that return
weak-form integrals, and free diagnostic tools---but must design its own
experimental strategy: choosing test functions, interpreting results,
diagnosing ill-conditioning, and iterating.  The task produces a
\emph{cognitive profile} spanning five faculties from the Burnell et
al.\ cognitive taxonomy: mathematical reasoning, metacognitive monitoring,
metacognitive control, executive planning, and cognitive flexibility.  Each
faculty is measured by a dedicated, mathematically grounded metric with
computable ground truth.  Across three OpenAI models (GPT-4o, GPT-5.4-mini,
GPT-5.4) and three difficulty levels, we find a clear capability hierarchy:
GPT-5.4 achieves near-perfect coefficient recovery at medium difficulty
(error $< 10^{-4}$) while GPT-4o fails at basic tool use on hard problems.
The benchmark reveals distinct behavioral failure modes---test function
homogeneity, premature termination, and over-reliance on consistency
checks---that map onto specific cognitive deficits.  All code, logs, and
data are publicly available.
\end{abstract}

%----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Introduction}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Benchmarks for scientific reasoning in large language models (LLMs) often
test whether the model can \emph{recall} known facts or \emph{solve}
textbook problems.  These tasks measure knowledge retrieval, not the
open-ended reasoning that characterizes real scientific work: forming
hypotheses, designing experiments to test them, interpreting results, and
iterating when the results are surprising.

Recent work on measuring progress toward AGI has called for benchmarks that
are \emph{targeted to specific cognitive abilities}, \emph{varied in
difficulty}, and \emph{varied in structure}~\citep{burnell2026cognitive}.
The Burnell et al.\ cognitive taxonomy identifies ten faculties underlying
general intelligence, including reasoning, metacognition, executive
functions, and problem solving.  Existing benchmarks cover some of these
faculties in isolation, but few provide a single task that engages multiple
faculties simultaneously with mathematically grounded evaluation.

We propose a benchmark built around a classical problem in applied
mathematics: \emph{identifying an unknown elliptic PDE from its solution}.
The model is given the solution $u(x)$ on a grid, a budget of queries that
probe the PDE through its weak formulation, and a set of computational
tools.  It must design a sequence of test functions, interpret the resulting
integrals, and recover the unknown coefficient functions.  The task is
\emph{inherently ill-conditioned} in a way that rewards scientific
reasoning: not all test functions are equally informative, and a model that
reasons about \emph{which measurements matter} will significantly
outperform one that collects data mechanically.

The benchmark produces a cognitive profile across five faculties, each
measured by a dedicated metric with computable ground truth:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Mathematical reasoning}: total coefficient recovery error.
    \item \textbf{Metacognitive monitoring}: does the model know which
          coefficients it is uncertain about?
    \item \textbf{Metacognitive control}: does the model target its queries
          toward its most uncertain coefficients?
    \item \textbf{Executive planning}: does the model iterate (multiple
          solves) or stop prematurely?
    \item \textbf{Cognitive flexibility}: can the model switch between test
          function families when its current approach stalls?
\end{enumerate}

%----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Problem Setup}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{The Unknown PDE}

The model is asked to identify the coefficient functions $a(x)$, $b(x)$,
$c(x)$, and $f(x)$ in the second-order elliptic boundary value problem
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pde}
    -\bigl(a(x)\, u'(x)\bigr)' + b(x)\, u'(x) + c(x)\, u(x) = f(x),
    \qquad x \in [0,1],
\end{equation}
with homogeneous Dirichlet conditions $u(0) = u(1) = 0$.  Each coefficient
is a linear combination of $N$ shifted Legendre polynomials:
\[
    a(x) = \sum_{j=0}^{N-1} a_j\, P_j(x),
    \qquad
    b(x) = \sum_{j=0}^{N-1} b_j\, P_j(x),
    \qquad \text{etc.}
\]
giving $4N$ unknowns total.  The coefficients are drawn randomly with
controlled sparsity and scale, and the PDE is solved numerically to produce
the solution $u(x)$ on a fine grid.

\subsection{Difficulty Levels}

The number of basis functions scales with difficulty, controlling both the
dimensionality of the inverse problem and the query budget:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Difficulty} & $N$ & \textbf{Unknowns} & \textbf{Budget} & \textbf{Turn limit} \\
\midrule
Easy    & 2 &  8 & 12 & 24 \\
Medium  & 3 & 12 & 18 & 36 \\
Hard    & 4 & 16 & 24 & 48 \\
Extreme & 8 & 32 & 48 & 96 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent The budget is set to $1.5\times$ the number of unknowns,
providing enough headroom for the model to solve the system, diagnose
problems, and iterate---but not so much that brute-force data collection
suffices.  The turn limit is $2\times$ the budget.

\subsection{Tools Available to the Model}

The model receives the following tools via a system prompt:

\paragraph{Query tool (\texttt{QUERY}).}
The model submits a test function $\varphi(x)$ as a symbolic expression
and receives the scalar $\int_0^1 f(x)\,\varphi(x)\dd x$.  Each query
costs one unit from the budget.  Internally, the system also computes and
stores the basis-weighted integrals
\[
    G^{\text{diff}}_{kj} = \int_0^1 P_j(x)\, u'(x)\, \varphi_k'(x)\dd x,
    \qquad
    G^{\text{adv}}_{kj} = \int_0^1 P_j(x)\, u'(x)\, \varphi_k(x)\dd x,
    \qquad
    G^{\text{react}}_{kj} = \int_0^1 P_j(x)\, u(x)\, \varphi_k(x)\dd x,
\]
for use by the solve tool.  The model never sees these matrices.

\paragraph{Solve tool (\texttt{COMPUTE: solve}).}
Assembles the $K \times 3N$ matrix $A = [G^{\text{diff}} \mid G^{\text{adv}}
\mid G^{\text{react}}]$ and solves the least-squares problem
$\min_\theta \|A\theta - r\|_2^2$ for the operator coefficients, and
separately recovers $f$ via $\min_f \|G^{\text{src}} f - r\|_2^2$.
Returns the recovered coefficient vectors and, after the first call, the
maximum per-coefficient change from the previous solve (enabling the model
to assess convergence).  Free and unlimited, but requires at least $4N$
stored queries.

\paragraph{Solution evaluation (\texttt{COMPUTE: eval\_solution}).}
Returns $u(x)$ and $u'(x)$ at arbitrary points.  Free.

\paragraph{Term integral tool (\texttt{COMPUTE: term\_integral}).}
Returns one of the three weight integrals ($\int u'\varphi'\dd x$,
$\int u'\varphi\dd x$, $\int u\varphi\dd x$) for a candidate test function,
allowing the model to assess informativeness \emph{before} spending a query.
Free.

\paragraph{Consistency check (\texttt{COMPUTE: check}).}
Compares the predicted left-hand side of~\eqref{eq:pde} (from estimated
$\hat a, \hat b, \hat c$) to the actual $\int f\varphi\dd x$ for a new
test function.  Costs one query.  The system warns the model when zero
discrepancy is expected due to exact determination.

\paragraph{Submission (\texttt{PREDICT}).}
Ends the session and submits the coefficients from the model's most recent
\texttt{COMPUTE: solve} as its answer.  The model decides \emph{when} to
stop, but the submitted answer always comes from the solver, eliminating
transcription errors.

\subsection{The Initial Prompt}

The standard prompt provides mathematical context about the task:

\begin{quote}
\small
\texttt{Note: in elliptic inverse problems of this type, the three
coefficient functions a(x), b(x), c(x) are not equally identifiable from
weak-form data. The difficulty of recovering each coefficient depends on
the magnitude of its contribution to the weak-form equations relative to
the others---and this varies with the solution u(x) and the test functions
you choose.}
\end{quote}

\noindent This tells the model \emph{what} is hard (unequal
identifiability) without telling it \emph{what to do about it}.
A baseline prompt without this context is available for ablation.

\section{The Ill-Conditioning Problem}
\label{sec:illcond}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

The weak form~\eqref{eq:pde} produces one linear equation per test
function, with three contributions on the left-hand side---diffusion,
advection, and reaction---weighted by the integrals that the
\texttt{term\_integral} tool returns.

For a typical test function, these weights have very different magnitudes.
The diffusion weight involves $u'(x)\varphi'(x)$, where both factors are
$O(1)$ for smooth functions.  The reaction weight involves
$u(x)\varphi(x)$, and since the solution satisfies homogeneous boundary
conditions and is often small ($|u| \sim 0.01$--$0.3$), this weight is
correspondingly small.  As a result:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item The source $f(x)$ is recovered essentially perfectly (error
          $\sim 10^{-11}$) because it appears linearly on the right-hand
          side.
    \item The diffusion coefficient $a(x)$ dominates every equation and is
          recovered with high accuracy.
    \item The advection coefficient $b(x)$ has moderate influence.
    \item The reaction coefficient $c(x)$ has tiny influence and is the
          hardest to recover---errors in $a$ and $b$ are absorbed into the
          estimate of $c$.
\end{itemize}

\noindent This creates a natural \emph{identifiability hierarchy}:
$f \gg a > b > c$.  A model that mechanically collects equations
will recover $f$ and $a$ but struggle with $b$ and $c$.  A model that
reasons about which test functions amplify the reaction weight will do
better on $c$.  This hierarchy is the core discriminating mechanism of
the benchmark.

%----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Cognitive Profile: Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

Following the cognitive taxonomy of Burnell et al.~\citep{burnell2026cognitive},
we define five metrics that together constitute a \emph{cognitive profile}
for each model.  A key advantage of this task is that the ground truth for
each metric is \emph{exactly computable} from the stored $G$-matrices at
every point in the session, without requiring human judgment.

\subsection{Mathematical Reasoning: Coefficient Error}

The primary outcome measure.  For each coefficient $j \in \{a, b, c, f\}$,
the error is the mean absolute deviation of the recovered Legendre
coefficients from the true values:
\[
    E_j = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=0}^{N-1} |\hat\theta_{j,i} - \theta_{j,i}^*|.
\]
The total error is $E = E_a + E_b + E_c + E_f$.  We also report pointwise
error $\max_{x \in [0,1]} |j^{\text{pred}}(x) - j^{\text{true}}(x)|$ for
each coefficient function.

\subsection{Metacognitive Monitoring: $M_k$}

After each \texttt{COMPUTE: solve}, the model reports its confidence that
each coefficient vector is within 0.1 of the true value:
\texttt{Confidence: a=XX\%, b=XX\%, c=XX\%, f=XX\%}.  The true epistemic
state is computable from the Fisher information: after $k$ queries, the
least-squares covariance is $(A_k^\top A_k)^{-1}$, giving per-block
uncertainty
\[
    \sigma_k(j) = \sqrt{\operatorname{mean}\!\bigl(
        \operatorname{diag}(A_k^\top A_k)^{-1}\big|_{\text{block }j}
    \bigr)},
    \qquad j \in \{a, b, c\}.
\]
Monitoring accuracy is the Spearman rank correlation between the model's
stated uncertainty $(1 - \hat c_k(j))$ and the true uncertainty
$\sigma_k(j)$:
\[
    M_k = \rho_S\!\bigl(\sigma_k,\, 1 - \hat c_k\bigr).
\]
$M_k = 1$ means the model correctly ranks which coefficients are most
uncertain.  $M_k \approx 0$ means no self-knowledge.  We report the
session mean $\bar M$.

\subsection{Metacognitive Control: $C_k$}

Monitoring is only useful if it drives behavior.  After query $k$, we
identify the most uncertain coefficient $j^* = \arg\max_j \sigma_k(j)$
and compute the information gain from query $k{+}1$ for each coefficient
via a Sherman--Morrison update on $(A_k^\top A_k)^{-1}$:
\[
    \mathrm{IG}_{k+1}(j) = \sigma_k(j) - \sigma_{k+1}(j).
\]
Control efficiency is:
\[
    C_k = \frac{\mathrm{IG}_{k+1}(j^*)}{\max_j\, \mathrm{IG}_{k+1}(j)}.
\]
$C_k = 1$ means the model chose the query that maximally reduces
uncertainty on its hardest coefficient.  We report the session mean
$\bar C$.

\subsection{Executive Planning: Solve Count and Budget Utilization}

A model that calls \texttt{COMPUTE: solve} multiple times and uses its
full query budget is exhibiting iterative experimental design---the
hallmark of scientific planning.  We report:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Solve count}: number of times \texttt{COMPUTE: solve}
          was called.
    \item \textbf{Budget utilization}: fraction of the query budget used
          (queries used / budget).
    \item \textbf{Check-to-query ratio}: fraction of budget spent on
          consistency checks vs.\ new queries.  High ratios indicate
          inhibitory control failure (the model wastes budget on
          uninformative verification instead of gathering new data).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Cognitive Flexibility: Test Function Diversity}

The system classifies each test function into one of four families:
polynomial ($x^m(1{-}x)^n$ products), trigonometric ($\sin$, $\cos$
combinations), exponential ($e^{\alpha x}$ weighted), and localized
(narrow-support functions).  A model that uses only polynomials---which
are nearly collinear in the weak-form integral space---is exhibiting
cognitive inflexibility.  We report the number of distinct families used
and the entropy of the family distribution.

\section{Experimental Design}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Models}

We evaluate three OpenAI models spanning two generations:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{GPT-4o}: the last major pre-reasoning-era model (2024).
    \item \textbf{GPT-5.4-mini}: the efficient tier of the current GPT-5
          family.
    \item \textbf{GPT-5.4}: the current frontier reasoning model.
\end{itemize}

\noindent All models use the standard prompt (with mathematical context),
temperature 0 (where available), and the same system prompt and tool
interface.

\subsection{Protocol}

Each model is evaluated at three difficulty levels (easy, medium, hard)
across two random seeds (42, 137), for a total of $3 \times 3 \times 2
= 18$ runs.  Budgets and turn limits are auto-scaled from difficulty.
Each run produces a JSON log containing: the full turn-by-turn transcript,
every query and its result, every solve with coefficient errors against
ground truth, every consistency check, every term integral call,
the post-hoc efficiency curve (error at each query count), and the
metacognitive metrics ($\sigma$-curves, monitoring reports, control
efficiency).

\paragraph{Finding 1: Clear generational hierarchy.}
At easy difficulty, all models succeed.  At medium difficulty, GPT-5.4
achieves error $< 0.002$ on both seeds, while GPT-5.4-mini collapses
to error $> 2$ and GPT-4o fails on one seed.  At hard difficulty, only
GPT-5.4 achieves sub-1.0 error (on seed 137).  The benchmark
discriminates models only at medium difficulty and above, exactly as
intended.

\paragraph{Finding 2: The $c(x)$ barrier.}
Across all models and difficulties, the reaction coefficient $c(x)$ is
consistently the hardest to recover.  On hard difficulty, $c(x)$ accounts
for 40--73\% of the total error.  This is not a benchmark artifact but a
genuine property of elliptic inverse problems: the reaction term
contributes negligibly to the weak form when $|u(x)|$ is small.

\paragraph{Finding 3: Behavioral divergence.}
The models exhibit qualitatively different strategies.  GPT-5.4 calls
\texttt{solve} 3--7 times per run, watches coefficients converge, and
uses 95--100\% of its budget.  GPT-5.4-mini typically solves once, often
burns budget on consistency checks, and stops early.  GPT-4o fails at
hard difficulty because it cannot reliably format expressions the parser
accepts (using \verb|^| instead of \verb|**|, or appending LaTeX markup).

\subsection{Metacognitive Metrics}

After each \texttt{COMPUTE: solve}, models report per-coefficient
confidence.  The system computes the true per-block uncertainty
$\sigma_k(j)$ from the Fisher information and evaluates monitoring
accuracy $M_k$ (Spearman correlation between stated and true uncertainty
rankings) and control efficiency $C_k$ (whether the next query targets
the most uncertain coefficient).

\emph{Monitoring accuracy.}  GPT-5.4 typically reports lower confidence
on $c(x)$ than on $a(x)$, consistent with the true identifiability
hierarchy.  GPT-5.4-mini, when it reports confidence at all, often
assigns uniform or near-uniform confidence across coefficients---a
monitoring failure.

\emph{Control efficiency.}  GPT-5.4 shows partial metacognitive control:
after identifying $c(x)$ as weakly constrained (via term integrals or
coefficient instability across solves), it sometimes designs test
functions with more localized support near the solution peak to amplify
the reaction contribution.  However, this behavior is inconsistent across
seeds, and even GPT-5.4 does not reliably close the full
monitoring--control loop.

\section{Discussion}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{What the Benchmark Measures}

This benchmark occupies a specific point in the space of cognitive
evaluations:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Not knowledge recall.}  The model is not asked to recite
          facts about PDEs.  It must \emph{use} the weak form to design
          experiments.
    \item \textbf{Not tool use alone.}  All models have identical tools.
          The difference is whether they use them \emph{strategically}.
    \item \textbf{Not arithmetic.}  The model never solves a linear system
          itself; \texttt{COMPUTE: solve} handles that.  The model's job
          is experimental design and epistemic management.
    \item \textbf{Multiple cognitive faculties.}  A single session engages
          mathematical reasoning, metacognitive monitoring and control,
          planning, and cognitive flexibility---producing a multi-dimensional
          cognitive profile rather than a scalar score.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Connection to the Cognitive Taxonomy}

Burnell et al.~\citep{burnell2026cognitive} identify metacognition as a
key faculty that existing benchmarks fail to cover adequately.  Our
benchmark addresses this gap by providing a task where the correct
metacognitive state is \emph{mathematically derivable} at every point in
the session.  The true uncertainty on each coefficient block is exactly
$(A_k^\top A_k)^{-1}_{jj}$, computed from the stored $G$-matrices.  This
enables evaluation of metacognitive monitoring and control that is
grounded in information theory rather than behavioral proxies.

The benchmark also engages executive functions---planning (the
solve--iterate--query loop), inhibitory control (resisting uninformative
consistency checks), and cognitive flexibility (switching test function
families)---and problem solving in the ``knowledge discovery'' sense:
designing experiments to uncover unknown structure.

\subsection{Limitations}

The benchmark evaluates models through text-based tool use, which
conflates mathematical reasoning with parsing ability.  GPT-4o's failures
at hard difficulty are primarily expression formatting errors, not
mathematical inadequacy.  Future versions could use structured tool
calling to separate these concerns.

The current evaluation uses only two seeds per difficulty level.  Hard
difficulty shows high variance (GPT-5.4 achieves 0.29 on one seed and
3.68 on the other), suggesting that some PDE instances are inherently
easier to identify.  A larger seed sweep would provide more robust
estimates.

Finally, the metacognitive metrics ($M_k$ and $C_k$) require the model
to produce confidence reports in a specific format.  Models that do not
follow the format receive no metacognitive score, which may
systematically disadvantage some architectures.

%----------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------

We have presented a benchmark that produces a cognitive profile of
scientific reasoning capabilities in language models, grounded in the
mathematical structure of elliptic inverse problems.  The benchmark
cleanly separates models by generation: GPT-5.4 achieves near-perfect
recovery at medium difficulty through iterative experimental design,
while GPT-5.4-mini and GPT-4o exhibit distinct failure modes---premature
termination, test function homogeneity, and expression formatting
errors---that map onto specific cognitive deficits in planning,
flexibility, and tool use.

The benchmark's key contribution is providing \emph{mathematically
grounded metacognitive evaluation}: the true epistemic state of the model
is exactly computable at every step, enabling precise measurement of
monitoring accuracy and control efficiency without behavioral proxies.
This addresses a gap identified by Burnell et al.\ in the current
landscape of cognitive evaluations for AI systems.
